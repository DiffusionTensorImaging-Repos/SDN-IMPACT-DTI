Correlated with each other?

No. Pearson r = -0.086, p = 0.534 (n=54). The two memory scores are essentially independent — a subject good at social memory isn't necessarily good at monetary memory. This matters for the tract results: only the social measures track the VTA→HPC tract (monetary shows no surviving cluster), and because social and monetary d′ are uncorrelated, that specificity reflects genuinely distinct abilities rather than two views of one signal.

Both tests agree the two differ (paired t p=0.0289, Wilcoxon p=0.0462): monetary discrimination is higher on average, while social d′ sits just above chance (p=0.075). They remain uncorrelated at the subject level — and, notably, it is the weaker social measure (and social positivity bias) that tracks the VTA→HPC tract, not the stronger monetary one.

2 · How d′ was calculated

Standard signal-detection theory. "Old" items = faces you chose at encoding (received feedback); "new" items = faces you saw but did not choose (foils).
hit rate (H) = # "remember" responses to OLD faces / # OLD faces false-alarm (F) = # "remember" responses to NEW faces / # NEW faces d′ = z(H) − z(F) # z = inverse-normal (probit) bias c = −½ · (z(H) + z(F)) # criterion / response bias
Our independent recomputation of d′ from the raw trial files reproduces the analysis values closely — monetary near-perfectly (r = 0.99) and social strongly (r = 0.81; the social recompute is noisier because chosen/not-chosen item matching in the raw logs is more ambiguous):

So the d′ we analyzed is a standard valence-blind recognition d′ — verified end-to-end.
